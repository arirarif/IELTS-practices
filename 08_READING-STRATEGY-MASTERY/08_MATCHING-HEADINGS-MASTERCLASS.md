# 08 — MATCHING HEADINGS — Full Masterclass

> Chris argues this is **harder than TFNG for most students** — but easy *if* you follow the one transformative technique below. He says this single tip has taken **hundreds, if not thousands** of his students from ~Band 6 → 8, or ~7 → 9. Worrying about these questions tends to wreck the rest of the test, so mastering it is critical.

---

## What it is & what it TESTS
- You get a list of **headings** (always **more headings than paragraphs**, to make it harder) and you match each **paragraph** to a heading.
- **Primary thing tested:** Can you understand the **GENERAL MEANING of each whole paragraph?** (NOT keywords, NOT individual sentences.)
- **Secondary thing tested:** Can you **differentiate between similar headings**? (Two or three headings will look alike in meaning.)

---

## The 4 common problems
1. **Too much information to process** + not enough time.
2. **Not spending enough time understanding the headings** and differentiating similar ones (impossible to answer if you don't fully understand the headings).
3. **Not spending enough time on the difference in meaning** → stress → time-wasting → snowball → students *hate* these.
4. **Not understanding the general meaning of the paragraphs** — the biggest problem (and exactly what's tested).

### What students WRONGLY do
- Read only the **first sentence** and **last sentence** of a paragraph, or
- Focus on **keywords** — the **opposite** of what works. You must grasp the **general meaning of the whole paragraph.**

---

## Tips (solving the problems)
- **Tip 1 — Do this question FIRST in its section.** Even if MCQ/other questions are printed first, **skip them and do matching headings first** — because it requires understanding the **whole text**, which then makes all the other questions in that section easier. Time-saver.
- **Tip 2 — Don't expect to read/understand every word.** It tests **general meaning**, not detail. An unknown word may not matter. (But don't *just* read a few words / skim too fast either.)
- **Tip 3 — When headings are similar:** physically **cover the others** (pen/ruler) to focus on the candidates, or **write the 2 similar headings side by side** so they're not lost in a sea of text — comparison gets much easier.
- **Tip 4 — If stuck, MOVE ON** and come back. Don't ruin later answers by spending 5–10 minutes on one. (Remember: Academic lets you get some wrong and still score 7–9.)

---

## ⭐ THE TRANSFORMATIVE TECHNIQUE: "Write your OWN heading first"
The core move that makes everything else easy:

### ✅ STEP-BY-STEP STRATEGY
1. **If matching headings is in the section, do it FIRST.**
2. **DO NOT look at the headings first.** (Repeat: ignore them entirely at the start — looking first → confusion + getting lost.)
3. **Read each paragraph and WRITE YOUR OWN heading for it** — *without* looking at the given headings. This **forces** you to understand the general meaning (you cannot title a paragraph without grasping its meaning) — i.e. you force your brain to do exactly what's tested.
   - Read **carefully but not in too much detail** (don't skim too fast — you'd waste time and learn nothing about the general meaning).
   - If a section is **2 or 3 paragraphs** under one heading, make **ONE** heading for the whole thing (decide the single most important idea; don't write three).
4. **THEN look at the given headings** for the first time. Read them; understand the **differences in meaning.**
5. **Match the obvious ones:** your self-made heading will often match a given heading **exactly or nearly** → that's the correct answer. Mark it.
6. For the rest: **close-read** the paragraph, compare the **meaning differences** between the shortlisted similar headings, and choose. Use elimination.
7. **If stuck, guess** (mark something / write an educated guess on the computer version) and **come back later.**

> **Why it's so powerful:** matching *your* headings to the official list is far easier than reading cold — "you'll be amazed how many you created are the same or nearly the same."

---

## 🧪 Worked example (the AI passage, Q27)
- Section = 3 paragraphs: (1) benefits of AI (predicts crime, heart attacks, given "imagination"); (2) AI is **better than humans** at forecasting, but humans **distrust** it; (3) to benefit from AI we must understand **why** people distrust it.
- **Your own single heading** (can't write three!): simplify → **"AI is better at many things than humans."**
- Look at given headings; match yours:
  - "Reasons we have more faith in human judgement than AI" → no (text gives no reasons we trust *humans more*) → eliminate.
  - "Widespread distrust of **an** AI **innovation**" → the singular "an innovation" doesn't fit (text is about many AI uses) → eliminate.
  - **"The superiority of AI projections over those made by humans"** → matches (good forecast, "almost always better at forecasting", superior) → ✅ **correct (heading 3).**
- **Anticipated wrong answers** (why they're traps): "recent cases show people don't like relying on AI" / "we should trust it more" → these are **details / individual sentences**, not the **general meaning of the whole paragraph.** Getting lost in detail = the classic mistake.

🇧🇩 **বাংলায়:** Matching Headings TFNG-এর চেয়েও কঠিন মনে হয় — কিন্তু এক কৌশলে সহজ: **আগে দেওয়া heading-গুলো দেখবে না।** প্রতিটা paragraph পড়ে **নিজে একটা heading লেখো** (এতে বাধ্য হয়েই paragraph-এর **general meaning** বুঝবে — এটাই টেস্ট করা হয়)। **তারপর** দেওয়া heading দেখো; তোমার heading-এর সাথে হুবহু/প্রায় মিলে যাওয়াগুলো = সঠিক উত্তর। এই question type **section-এ আগে** করো (পুরো text পড়তে হয় বলে বাকি প্রশ্ন সহজ হয়)। keyword বা প্রথম/শেষ লাইনে আটকে থেকো না — **পুরো paragraph-এর মানে** দেখো। আটকে গেলে আন্দাজ করে পরে ফিরে এসো।
