# 07 — TRUE/FALSE/NOT GIVEN & YES/NO/NOT GIVEN — Full Masterclass

> The "dreaded" question type and the one students worry about most. This is the complete VIP treatment: what it tests, the common problems, the tips, the **personalisation technique**, and the **step-by-step strategy** with a worked example.

---

## PART A — TRUE / FALSE / NOT GIVEN

### What it asks
Read statements; in relation to the text, decide each is **True**, **False**, or **Not Given**. TFNG is about **FACT** (e.g. "this pen is purple" — a fact).

### The 4 key problems
1. **Misinformation** — it's the most-feared type, so it has the most online content; most of those teachers aren't examiners → huge confusion.
2. **Not understanding what "Not Given" means** → people **search for something that isn't there** (like hunting a room for an object that isn't in it) → wastes time → wrecks the *other* questions too (less time for them).
3. **Failing to understand the whole sentence** — because of the **keyword strategy** (keywords-keywords-keywords). Keywords are *sometimes* useful but mostly not; over-focusing means you miss the sentence's real meaning.
4. **Getting lost in the text** wasting time (the keyword trap again).

### Tips before the strategy (don't skip these)
- **Tip 1 — Ignore previous knowledge / common sense.** Use **only** the text. (Don't use outside facts or "the logical answer.") You **cannot** get Band 9 "without reading the text."
- **Tip 2 — Identify QUALIFYING words** that qualify nouns: **some vs all**, **many**, **polar bear vs brown bear**, **female polar bear vs male**. These test whether you understand the *whole sentence* or just a keyword.
- **Tip 3 — There will always be at least one True, one False, and one Not Given.** So if you're confident on the others, the leftover can be deduced (e.g. T, F, F → the 4th is likely Not Given).
- **Tip 4 — Don't rely solely on skim/scan.** Skim = general meaning; scan = location of keywords; but the main skill tested is **CLOSE READING**. Skim/scan are the *opposite* of close reading.
- **Tip 5 — Be aware of synonyms** (e.g. *bone* → *skeleton / skeletal system*). If you hunt for the exact word you may miss it or land in the wrong place.
- **Tip 6 — If you can't find the answer, write "NG" beside it** and move on; come back later with spare time to re-check. (It's *probably* Not Given anyway.)

### 🧠 THE PERSONALISATION TECHNIQUE (removes all "Not Given" confusion)
Practise this at home with pen & paper, using **facts about YOURSELF** (you remember things related to you):
1. Write a **Question Statement** = a fact about you (name, age, nationality). *e.g. "Chris is 40 years old."*
2. Write a **TRUE** statement that agrees with it. *"Chris was born on 16 December 1982."* (They agree → **True**.)
3. Write a **FALSE** statement that disagrees. *"Chris is 21."* (Contradicts → **False**.)
4. Write a **NOT GIVEN** statement — related but **not enough information** to judge. *"Chris is middle-aged."* (Doesn't clearly agree or disagree — could be 40 or 55 → **Not Given**.)
- Second example: Fact "Chris has Irish nationality." True: "born on the island of Ireland, has an Irish passport." False: "Chris is Vietnamese." Not Given: "Chris is married to a Vietnamese woman and his sons have Vietnamese passports" — *is he* Vietnamese? **We don't know → Not Given.**
- **Crucial:** Do **NOT** import outside knowledge (e.g. "you can't get Vietnamese citizenship if not born there"). **Only the text counts.**
- Do this **2–3 times for yourself** → on test day, when confused, anchor to: *"I am X years old / I am a citizen of Y"* and the logic becomes obvious.

### The decision logic (keep it SIMPLE)
- **First: is it there at all?** If not → **Not Given.**
- **If it's there:** does the meaning **match** → **True**; can you **correct/contradict** it (text says yellow, statement says purple) → **False**.
- **One technique for False:** if you can **correct the statement** to make it true, it's **False**. If you can't (it's already the same) → **True**.

### ✅ STEP-BY-STEP STRATEGY (follow exactly; don't half-do it)
1. **Read instructions** — confirm TFNG (not YNNG; strategy differs slightly).
2. **Read each whole statement** — understand it; **don't** highlight keywords. Note **qualifying words**.
3. Think of **possible synonyms** that may appear in the text.
4. **Match the statement to the correct part of the text** (scan for words/synonyms) — **location only**, not the answer yet.
5. **Re-read the statement**, then **close-read** that text section carefully.
6. **Hone in on the exact words** that decide it; **decide** True/False/Not Given.
7. Move on; repeat for each.

### 🐻 Worked example (polar bears)
- Statement: *"Polar bears suffer from various health problems due to the build-up of fat under their skin."*
- Scan → find "fat under their skin" location (paragraph about climate change & 11cm of fat).
- Close-read: text says humans with that much "adipose tissue" (= fat) would be obese/diabetic, **"yet the polar bear experiences no such consequences."**
- Statement says they *suffer*; text says **no consequences** → they **don't agree** → **FALSE.**

## PART B — YES / NO / NOT GIVEN

### What it asks
Same format, but decide whether statements agree/disagree with **the WRITER'S opinion/views** (not facts). *"I believe purple pens are better than blue"* = opinion.

### Key problems
1. **Confusing TFNG (fact) with YNNG (opinion).**
2. **Not understanding the writer's opinion** — if you only hunt for facts, you can't judge opinion.
3. Same as TFNG: **wasting time** looking for something that's not there.
4. **Confusing the writer's opinion with other people's opinions** in the text (e.g. "Chris believes X but Jason believes Y") → if it's only *Jason's* view and the writer doesn't comment → **Not Given**.

### Strategy
> **Exactly the same as TFNG — but focus on the WRITER'S OPINION, not fact.**
- **Yes** = text/writer **agrees** with the statement's opinion.
- **No** = writer **disagrees**.
- **Not Given** = no information to judge the **writer's** view.

- **Don't** think watching a short clip = mastery. **You need the strategy + slow practice + mastery → automatic on test day.**

🇧🇩 **বাংলায়:** TFNG = **তথ্য (fact)**; YNNG = **লেখকের মতামত (opinion)** — দুটো গুলিয়ে ফেলো না। নিয়ম: আগে দেখো তথ্যটা **আছে কিনা** — না থাকলে **Not Given**; থাকলে মিললে **True/Yes**, বিরোধ করলে (বা statement-টা "শুদ্ধ" করা গেলে) **False/No**। শুধু keyword নয়, **পুরো বাক্যের মানে** বোঝো; **qualifying words** (some/all, female/male) খেয়াল করো; **synonym**-এর জন্য প্রস্তুত থাকো; বাইরের জ্ঞান ব্যবহার করো না — শুধু **text**। নিজের সম্পর্কে fact দিয়ে T/F/NG লেখার **personalisation technique** বাসায় ২–৩ বার অনুশীলন করো। সবচেয়ে বড় শিক্ষা: **"Not Given" লেখার সাহস** রাখো।
