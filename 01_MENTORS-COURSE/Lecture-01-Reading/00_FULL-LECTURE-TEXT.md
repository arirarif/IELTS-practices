## PAGE 2 — Reading Test Structure
The **READING** test of the IELTS exam varies in its contents for the Academic and General Training test takers. In short, the exam will consist of three reading passages, which may include pictures, graphs, tables or diagrams. Each passage will have several different types of questions, which may be printed either before or after the passage. Often the texts and the questions are arranged in the order of difficulty; however, this may not always be the case.

The passages that the students are likely to face in the exams are taken from books, magazines, newspapers and journals and are non-specialist, meaning that no specialist knowledge will be required from the test taker. There will be **40 questions, equaling 40 marks** that will later be converted into a band score of 9. All in all, students may expect to see 9 different types of questions in the passages, namely:

1. Multiple Choice Questions (MCQ)
2. Short Answer Questions
3. Summary Completion
4. Sentence Completion
5. Matching Headings to individual paragraphs
6. Yes, No, Not Given / True, False, Not Given Statements
7. Classification Questions
8. Diagram Labeling
9. Selecting Factors / Phrases
*(handwritten add: Matching Information to individual paragraph)*

**General Hints on Reading**
- Read as widely as you can, e.g. newspapers, journals, specialist magazines, so that you develop the skills of reading with speed outside your subject area.
- As you read, look for patterns in the organization of the texts.
- Train yourself to read to understand the meaning of the passage rather than just reading the words.
- As you read, always practice predicting what you are about to read.
- Learn to let go the unfamiliar words, and look at the overall meaning.
- Spend a specific period each day reading.
- Practice and only practice will guarantee you a good score in the Reading Section of the IELTS exam. So, practice as much as you can!

## PAGE 3 — Basic Problems / Reading Speed
**Basic Problems**
- **Misreading instructions:** Make sure you read the instructions carefully.
- **Timing:** Learn to work out what the question you are doing requires, and find each answer as quickly as possible.
- **Topics:** The test is designed to test your reading comprehension skills, not your knowledge of any particular subject.
- **Answer sheets:** You must complete the answer sheet within 60 minutes.

**READING SPEED — Types of Reading (according to the purpose of reading):**
1. **Study Reading** — where serious concentration is required, e.g. reading of course books such as *Hamlet*. Approximate speed 200–300 words per minute; comprehension 80–90%.
2. **Average Reading** — where it is not important to follow an argument closely, e.g. reading a novel for pleasure. Approximate speed 250–300 words per minute; comprehension ~70%.
3. **Skimming** — where reading time is fixed, and it is only essential to get the general idea of what the reading material is all about. Expected speed ~800 words per minute; comprehension ~50%.

**4 MAIN SKILLS FOR EFFICIENT & EFFECTIVE READING**
1. **Previewing** (2 minutes for each passage) — speed-read / preview the passage to get its subject and overall meaning. While previewing, especially read Titles, Headings, Illustrations, Diagrams, and any print in bold type or italics. Get a **"Bird's Eye View"** before going into details.

---

## PAGE 4 — The other 3 skills
2. **Interpreting the instructions and questions** (2 minutes) — understand:
   (a) what form the answer takes (gap-filling / multiple choice / matching information etc.)
   (b) the type of answer according to the question requirements — specific or general
   (c) the answer category (a number / date / reason etc.)
3. **Scanning the text for specific answers** (1 minute per question) — after previewing, scan to find which paragraph holds the answer, and locate the relevant Text, Table, Diagram or Graph.
4. **Working out Unfamiliar Vocabulary** — 5 techniques:
   - **T1 — Check the context:** look at the words before and after the unknown word.
   - **T2 — Look for a definition:** signal words like *is, means, refers to, that is, consists of*. e.g. "Snoring **is** a Noise" → 'is' signals a definition.
   - **T3 — Identify the word's place & purpose:** what form is it — Noun, Adjective, Verb or Adverb?
   - **T4 — Look for connective words:** they hint the relationship/meaning.
   - **T5 — Break the word into syllables:** Common Roots, Prefix, Suffix help you guess the meaning.

## PAGE 5 — General Instructions for Test Taking (7 points)
1. **Passage** and **Instruction** carefully read; first read the passage instruction quickly. *(Read the passage instruction once quickly to know what's being asked.)*
2. **Reading Text** — note any sentence in a **negative sense**: *No, None, Not, Rarely, Merely, Difficulty, Barely.*
3. **Reading Text** — note signal words: *Often, Hence, Sometimes, Somewhat, However.*
4. **Multiple Choice:** if you know the correct answer, mark it; but read **all** options — sometimes a better option appears later. In IELTS, mark only **one**.
5. **Sentence Completion / Fill in the blanks:** if the instruction says *"Using words taken from the text"*, use the exact words — but you may need to change the **form**.
6. **Each Section:** check whether the answers are in order; manage your time.
7. If a blank needs **more than 1 word**, use the **appropriate form**. Check everything before the exam ends.

### Exercise passage — "Sky's the limit"
> *Airlines are making money again, but they are also making their growing numbers of passengers miserable. They need to find ways of doing one without the other.*
>
> Civil aviation is a tale of two contrasting trends: on the one hand, there is strong growth in sales and profits for the world's airlines. On the other side of the coin, there is increasing misery and dissatisfaction for travelers, especially in America and Europe.
>
> For an industry that collectively lost $15 billion in the first few years of the 1990s, the past few years have brought a welcome change. The new profits of all scheduled airlines worldwide rose from $4.5 billion in 1995, when the boom began, to $8.5 billion two years later. However, the airlines are having to work harder and harder for them. The average consumer is paying 20% less per passenger-mile in real terms than 20 years ago, and revenue per seat is declining by an average of 2% per year.
>
> At the same time, consumer dissatisfaction with air travel has reached a new peak in America… Hollywood has even started making movies that incorporate the frustrations of air travel in the plot. The prospect of airline mergers is met with even greater hostility. Seeing what a poor job the airlines are doing, the public refuse to believe that bigger will mean better.
>
> Moreover, the airlines are now being attacked by environmentalists for contributing to global warming as well as causing noise pollution around airports. Although jet engines today are much quieter than they were ten years ago, the huge rise in the number of flights makes the problem more pervasive.
>
> To cap it all, consumer groups are now attacking airlines for squashing in too many people. Sitting still for long periods in cramped conditions, they say, can cause blood clots. In response, some carriers are taking out a few rows of seats to create more space for economy passengers to move around in. But dissatisfaction still keeps rising.
>
> Why is the industry failing to keep its customers happy? One answer is that it has been growing too fast. Even in a supposedly mature market such as America, the airlines, and the air-transport infrastructure of airports and traffic control, have simply not been able to keep up. Although the airlines like to say that 70% of delays are caused by weather (i.e. not their fault), many of these delays could in fact be avoided or shortened, if the system worked better.
>
> But consumer pressure in response to gridlocked skies is only one force for change in the air travel industry to be faced in this century. Equally important are the parallel trends of privatization and liberalization which are proving unstoppable.

### Exercise passage — "Classic style" (*For a few short years, fins were in fashion on American cars*)
> It's rare to see fins on the back of motor cars today… But for a decade or two in the years after the Second World War, the inclusion of ever more extravagant and ostentatious fins was the height of fashion among American car designers and the must-have automotive accessory for the discerning car buyer.
>
> It started in 1947 when chief of styling at the car-making firm of General Motors, Harley Earl, developed the entirely new notion of attaching fins to the back of the company's motor cars, typically on the edges of the trunk, or boot, running down to the vehicle's brake lights. Earl had been inspired by the twin tail fins he had seen on the **Lightning fighter planes** used during the war and instructed General Motors' team of designers to play around with the same concept. The designers liked the idea immediately – perhaps unsurprisingly, could there be any better symbol of speed and power? And after some experimentation, the first General Motors' Cadillac was released the following year sporting a pair of relatively modest fins. The effect was immediate: the public loved the new innovation – the young and young at heart especially – and competing firms were forced quite literally to go back to the drawing board. So, in the 1950s, a race began between American car manufacturers to see who could produce cars with the most pronounced, extreme and even outlandish fins. It seemed almost impossible to overdo it as consumers rushed to the showroom to buy the latest model and keep one step ahead.
>
> It's necessary to understand the culture of the times in America… they served no practical purpose whatsoever; these were not the 'spoilers' that were later attached to cars to improve aerodynamics, road handling and fuel economy. They existed simply to amplify the shape of the car, to accentuate its image, speed and style. And a culture had emerged… in the 1950s and 60s the American people were filled with a sense of **national optimism** because theirs was a young country, the economy was booming and their place in the world was assured. Furthermore, iron ore was cheap, as were the coal and oil necessary to turn it into steel, so car production costs were a fraction of what they are today. The result was extravagant cars: General Motors' **Firebird III** had no fewer than nine fins – still a world record – while the nearly six-metre long Eldorado… the tallest fin was nearly 300mm high.
>
> Of course, it couldn't last. By the 1980s, American society had become concerned about petrol consumption, road safety and car efficiency. In short, people wanted a different type of car. The result was that the Federal Government passed a number of **new laws** that transformed the automotive industry. Cars became safer and greener, but some of the flair and individualism has arguably been lost… And one final point: in 1998, Chinese researchers found a fossil of a species they named the **abnormal shrimp**. This was a two-metre long predator… on its tail it had a series of fins that the researchers have been able to attribute no practical purpose whatsoever.

4. Which model of car had the most number of fins ever?
5. In the 1980s, what was introduced to make motoring more expensive?
6. According to Chinese research in 1998, what creature once had fins?

### Type 2 — Summary **without** word box → Exercise: "In pursuit of the perfect Tomato"
> For supermarket shoppers, the tomato symbolizes what has been lost in terms of taste and texture, in exchange for being able to buy cheap vegetables and fruit all year round. But a fight-back for flavour is under way – its champion is Harry Klee, horticulture professor at the University of Florida. 'The big problem with the modern commercial tomato is that growers are not paid for flavor, they're paid for **yield and extended shelf life**,' says Klee. His answer is 'an integrated system that starts with the **consumer** and what they want.'
>
> Klee's research started with **heritage** tomatoes (from before mass commercialization) and compared these with modern varieties. The loss of flavor coincided with the intensive breeding that began in the second half of the twentieth century. 'Since flavor started going down, yields have gone up by three hundred per cent.' Biochemical analysis identified sixty-eight flavor compounds; the most important are 'volatiles', many of which also contribute strongly to the enticing **smell** of freshly-picked tomatoes. Some chemicals (cis-3-hexanal) thought important were not, while others such as **geranial**, regarded as marginal, were actually important.
>
> Scientists are also discovering the genetics of flavor and durability. One mutation, favored because it gives ripe tomatoes a beautifully even scarlet surface, reduces flavoring compounds. The Florida researchers have bred a tomato half-way between old and modern lines: 'People love the taste of this **hybrid** and it is easier to grow… a hundred per cent of the flavor of old varieties and eighty per cent of the performance of modern ones.'
>
> Although Klee worked for a chemical company until 1995 developing **GM** crops, he does not see a role for GM technology here, because of public resistance and costly regulatory approval. 'We can do it through conventional breeding, using modern genetics and flavor chemistry.'
